EchoGuide – Accessible Museum Companion

Master project · UX · Accessibility · Iterative prototyping

EchoGuide explores how museums can become more inclusive for visitors with visual impairments and other access needs. The project started from a concrete case brief about digital information screens at a Viking ship museum and grew into a broader concept for an audio-based companion app.

Design sprint UX Accessibility Speculative design Prototyping p5.js

Context & challenge

The initial case asked for a digital info screen to show the day’s activities, film screenings and events at the museum. Rather than simply digitising an analogue magnet board, we asked:

  • What if we designed from the perspective of visitors who cannot rely on vision?
  • How would museum information change if 40% of visitors were blind?

This shift led us towards speculative and accessibility-focused explorations, moving beyond screens to consider audio, tactility and independence.

My role

  • Co-developed the overall concept and user scenarios.
  • Contributed to storyboards and early speculative prototypes.
  • Worked on interface and interaction decisions for the app-based solution.
  • Participated in testing and reflection on accessibility and usability.

Process & iterations

Iteration 1 — Speculative “Smart Cane”

We initially explored a speculative physical artefact: a cane that could sense and read information in the museum through tactile feedback and text-to-Braille translation. This helped us understand technological and practical limitations, but also highlighted the value of independence and non-intrusive support.

Iteration 2 — App prototype with audio feedback

We then shifted towards a mobile app built in p5.js using image recognition and audio output to read exhibit information aloud. This iteration focused on:

  • Camera-based scanning of markers or images near exhibits.
  • Audio narration of text associated with each object.
  • Basic layout and navigation for visually impaired users.

Iteration 3 — Usability & scalability

In the final iteration, we refined the concept with more attention to:

  • Scalability to other museums and contexts.
  • Usefulness beyond visual impairment (e.g. dyslexia, children, elderly visitors).
  • Opportunities for personalised narratives using AI in future work.

Outcome & impact

EchoGuide became a concept for an accessible museum companion that:

  • Supports independent navigation by reading information aloud.
  • Can be adapted to different exhibitions and institutions.
  • Serves multiple user groups with different access needs.

While the technical prototype remains exploratory, the project offers concrete design directions for museums wanting to move beyond purely visual communication.

Reflections & learnings

EchoGuide strengthened my understanding of accessibility as an integral part of UX rather than an add-on. The process also showed the value of speculative first iterations to open up the design space before converging on more feasible solutions.
