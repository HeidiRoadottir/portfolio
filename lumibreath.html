LumiBreath – Interactive Breathing Lamp

Digital Designer course · Tangible interaction · Wellbeing technology

LumiBreath is a physical-digital lamp that guides users through calming breathing exercises using light and motion. The project explores how embodied interaction and everyday objects can support stress reduction and emotional regulation.

Interaction design Embodied interaction Physical prototyping Arduino / microcontroller Design research

Context & challenge

Stress and anxiety are increasingly common, particularly among young people. At the same time, much of the technology we use in everyday life is designed for productivity rather than calmness. LumiBreath asks:

  • How can a familiar object like a lamp become a subtle guide for breathing?
  • What happens when we design for bodily awareness rather than screen time?

The project draws on third-wave HCI and theories of embodiment to integrate technology into lived experience in a gentle, sensory way.

My role

  • Co-designed the overall concept and interaction.
  • Contributed to the theoretical grounding and problem framing.
  • Worked on mapping breathing patterns to light and motion behaviour.
  • Helped document iterations and reflections for the final report.

Design & interaction

Breathing rhythm

The lamp uses slow changes in brightness and movement to indicate when to inhale, hold and exhale. The goal was to create a rhythm that can be followed without cognitive overload or constant visual attention.

Materiality & form

The physical form was chosen to blend into a home environment while still signalling that it is more than “just” a normal lamp. Light diffusion and softness of motion were key parameters to avoid a clinical or gadget-like feel.

Embodied experience

Instead of treating interaction as button presses, the project treats breathing as the central interaction. The lamp becomes a companion in a small ritual of self-regulation, rather than a device demanding attention.

Outcome & reflections

The final prototype demonstrates how everyday artefacts can be designed to support mental wellbeing. Testing and reflection suggested that:

  • Subtle, slow visual cues are more calming than explicit instructions.
  • Users appreciate when technology “fits into” existing routines and spaces.
  • Designing for embodiment requires combining technical and philosophical perspectives.

For me, LumiBreath strengthened my interest in designing technologies that engage the body, emotions and context – not just the screen.
